# HPSR Epistemology Skill

Health Policy and Systems Research (HPSR) operates with a different epistemology from clinical medicine and laboratory science. This skill teaches the agent to recognise, retrieve, evaluate, and synthesise HPSR evidence appropriately.

## Evidence Confidence Scoring (Required)

For all synthesised evidence, you must assign an explicit **Evidence Confidence Score** based on the following:

- **High Confidence:** Systematic reviews with meta-analysis (if appropriate), large-scale robustly designed implementation studies, or high-quality grey literature from authoritative institutional sources (e.g., WHO, National Health Mission). Evidence is consistent, recent, and highly context-relevant.
- **Medium Confidence:** Well-conducted observational studies, case studies, or policy analyses with clear methodology and reflexivity. Evidence is contextually relevant but potentially limited by scope or design.
- **Low Confidence:** Narrative reviews, opinion pieces, older data without clear context, or studies lacking explicit methodological rigour.

Additionally, for every source, explicitly categorize it:
- **Peer-Reviewed Systematic Review**
- **Primary Institutional Report** (Government, WHO, etc.)
- **Peer-Reviewed Empirical Study**
- **Expert Analysis/Opinion**

Weighting must account for both the confidence score and the source type.

## What HPSR Is

HPSR is an interdisciplinary field that studies health systems as complex adaptive systems. It draws on sociology, political science, economics, anthropology, organisational theory, and public administration alongside epidemiology and clinical science. Its core questions are:

- How do health systems actually function (as opposed to how they are designed to function)?
- Why do health policies and programmes succeed or fail in implementation?
- Who benefits and who is excluded, and why?
- How do context, actors, and power shape health outcomes?
- What does "scaling up" mean and when does it work?

HPSR is inherently context-sensitive. A finding from one health system does not automatically transfer to another. This is not a limitation -- it is the central epistemic feature of the field.

## Evidence Hierarchy in HPSR

The conventional biomedical evidence hierarchy (RCT at the top, expert opinion at the bottom) does not apply straightforwardly in HPSR. Use the following framework instead:

**For effectiveness questions** ("does intervention X improve outcome Y?"):
- The standard hierarchy broadly applies, but note that RCTs are rare in health systems research and often inappropriate for complex interventions
- Controlled before-after studies, interrupted time series, and natural experiments are often the strongest feasible designs
- Systematic reviews of implementation evidence (not just efficacy evidence) are particularly valuable

**For implementation and process questions** ("how and why does X work or not work?"):
- Qualitative research is primary, not supplementary
- Realist synthesis and realist evaluation are appropriate review methods
- Case studies, ethnographies, and process evaluations contain essential evidence
- Programme theory and logic models are part of the evidence base

**For policy and political economy questions** ("who decided what, and why?"):
- Policy analysis, document analysis, and key informant interviews are the appropriate methods
- Historical and institutional analysis is relevant
- Grey literature (government reports, programme evaluations, audit reports) often contains evidence unavailable in journals

**Do not** apply a biomedical hierarchy to HPSR questions. A well-conducted qualitative study answering an implementation question is stronger evidence than a poorly-conducted RCT answering a different question.

## Qualitative Evidence Appraisal

When retrieving and evaluating qualitative studies, apply the CASP Qualitative Checklist logic:

1. Was there a clear statement of the aims of the research?
2. Is a qualitative methodology appropriate for the question?
3. Was the research design appropriate to address the aims?
4. Was the recruitment strategy appropriate to the aims?
5. Was the data collected in a way that addressed the research issue?
6. Has the relationship between researcher and participants been adequately considered (reflexivity)?
7. Have ethical issues been taken into consideration?
8. Was the data analysis sufficiently rigorous?
9. Is there a clear statement of findings?
10. How valuable is the research? (transferability, not generalisability)

Note: qualitative research is assessed on credibility, transferability, dependability, and confirmability -- not internal and external validity in the quantitative sense.

## Mixed Methods Evidence

Mixed methods studies are common and important in HPSR. When appraising them:

- Identify whether the quantitative and qualitative components are integrated at the design, collection, or interpretation stage (integration matters)
- Note whether the mixed methods approach was justified given the research question
- Assess each component using the appropriate framework (CASP qualitative or quantitative as relevant)
- Look for whether the mixing adds understanding beyond either component alone

## Implementation Science Frameworks

HPSR research often uses theoretical frameworks to explain how and why things work. Recognise and note when papers use:

- **Consolidated Framework for Implementation Research (CFIR)** -- inner/outer setting, intervention characteristics, individual characteristics, process
- **PARIHS / i-PARIHS** -- evidence, context, facilitation
- **Realist evaluation** -- context-mechanism-outcome (CMO) configurations
- **Health Systems Building Blocks** (WHO) -- service delivery, health workforce, health information, medical products, financing, leadership/governance
- **Complex Adaptive Systems** framing
- **Political economy analysis** frameworks

If a paper uses a recognised framework, flag it and summarise which components of the framework the paper addresses.

## Grey Literature Is Primary, Not Supplementary

In HPSR, grey literature is often more important than journal articles for certain questions:

- **Programme evaluations** -- often commissioned by government or donors, not published in journals
- **Policy documents** -- the actual text of policies, schemes, and missions (e.g., National Health Mission operational guidelines, NHP 2017, Ayushman Bharat documentation)
- **Government data** -- NFHS, DLHS, HMIS, SRS, NITI Aayog reports
- **Parliamentary records** -- standing committee reports on health, CAG audit reports
- **Institutional reports** -- WHO, World Bank, PHFI, NHSRC, IIPS, state NHM documentation

Do not treat absence from PubMed as absence of evidence. For many HPSR questions, the most important evidence is not in any journal.

## Geographic and Contextual Weighting

For India and South Asia-specific queries:

- Weight evidence from India and comparable LMICs more heavily than evidence from high-income countries
- Note when a paper is from a different health system and flag the transferability question explicitly
- Recognise that Indian health system research has a growing body of literature in journals like the National Medical Journal of India, Indian Journal of Medical Research, Economic and Political Weekly (health section), and the Journal of Health Management
- EPW (Economic and Political Weekly) publishes important health systems and policy analysis that is often not indexed in PubMed -- search it explicitly for Indian policy topics

## Power, Equity, and Positionality

HPSR is explicitly attentive to power and equity in ways that clinical research often is not. When synthesising HPSR evidence:

- Note whether studies disaggregate findings by gender, caste, class, religion, geography, or disability
- Flag studies that attend to power dynamics between providers and patients, or between state and communities
- Note whether community perspectives, not just provider or institutional perspectives, are represented
- If a study focuses only on supply-side factors, flag that demand-side and community factors may not be captured

## Output Conventions for HPSR Synthesis

When producing literature reviews or research briefs for HPSR topics:

- Organise by theme and conceptual category, not just by study design
- Explicitly discuss what is known, what is contested, and what has not been studied
- Distinguish between efficacy evidence (controlled settings), effectiveness evidence (routine conditions), and implementation evidence (how it was delivered)
- Use the phrase "context matters" only when you have specified which contextual factors matter and why -- vague appeals to context are not useful
- Flag when evidence is dominated by a particular geography, methodology, or funder perspective
- Note when the field has moved on from a prior consensus

## Do Not Do These Things

- Do not apply biomedical RCT logic to implementation or policy questions
- Do not dismiss qualitative studies as "just anecdote"
- Do not treat a systematic review as automatically superior to a well-conducted primary study on a question the review did not fully address
- Do not assume high-income country evidence transfers to LMIC settings without stating the assumption explicitly
- Do not ignore grey literature
- Do not treat absence of evidence as evidence of absence in a field where grey literature dominates
